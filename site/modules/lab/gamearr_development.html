>> GAMEARR_DEVELOPMENT://GENERAL LAB

Developing a Video Game PVR to fit the *Arr ecosystem.

>> Introductions

This entry is going to be a little less clinical and documentative than usual. I want to start this off by stating that I am not by any means an application developer. At best, I am a web-end UI/UX programmer. I've dabbled in a few programming languages, I've written some scripts for around the homelab, altered config files, and I've written a few mock programs, calculators, calendars, etc. But I've never really had the desire or need to learn application programming, software. I work closely with hardware which is what I enjoy doing, hence the homelab. Though, roughly two weeks ago now, I had the passing thought. "I don't really have much I want to add to the servers, every new big project I want to do would cost money I don't have with new technologies and hardware... I wonder if there's an *arr for video games." And to my surprise, not really. I only saw a few projects, and none of them did quite what I wanted to.


I mean no shade or offense to the developers of Questarr or Playerr, but they have a more limited scope than what I am imagining for Gamearr. Both projects lack in the "diversity" of game preservation. Questarr works almost exclusively with Prowlarr/Torznab, and Playerr does the same with more handheld-specific features. Torznab and Newznab (Torrents and Usenet) have a lot, but they don't have everything or the best versions of what's out there - especially in regards to legacy consoles. There's self-hosted library managers like RomM, GameVault, Gameyfin that do great at showing your existing game library, but don't do any of the aquisition. There's Lutris for the desktop, its a very powerful tool, but that isn't built for a homelab, it's not designed to run in a container on a server 24/7.


Point being, there isn't an existing software that offers what I envision the end-goal v1.0 of Gamearr to be. The all-in-one Video Game PVR solution. Rather than just relying on Torznab and Newznab, video game preservation is largely done outside of that. Direct Download ROM sites, TSV sheets, etc etc. I'm trying to build the tool that does it all. The first "mock up" v0.1 was just a CLI NoPayStation PS Vita parser, search the title, find the one that matched what you wanted by region, then pull it and extract it. No metadata, no fancy UI, just moving data. The test of "is this something achievable." It went well so in v0.2, I added a queue system, and added support for PSP and PSX which NPS has the TSV files for, added a very barebones SQLite database. In v0.3, I made it docker-compatible with background runners, and at this point was still CLI based. v0.4, I build the core of the Web UI transitioning to FastAPI, expanded the SQLite, automated the Metadata fetch through the IGDB API while trying to respect their servers in the process. And once I had a functional demo, flipped the repo to public. I got some valid criticisms for the quality of the code, and worked hard to refactor it so it meets the production ready standards that the *arr community expects from such a project. I'm testing and wrapping up the backend portion of v0.5 before I tackle the UI refactor; this time the big improvement is NAS and ARM support.


I've been learning as I go, with each step of development, Gamearr becomes more stable, more refined, I'm having to look up less and less how to do certain things. It's becoming less forums, YouTube tutorials, and outdated Python 2 programming instructions and more programming. Which honestly, I'm very glad for. Here soon in v0.6 I'll be working on preparing the backend for nearly full platform support. Everything from the Dreamcast and Sega Saturn hopefully all the way up to the PS4 and Xbox 360. Every console has it's own unique requirements, methods of preservation, metadata parsing, etc. But I also think it's a perfect spot for Gamearr to really shine. I'm not building it for the 'low-hanging fruit' of popular formats. It's being built for the edge cases - the encrypted, the fragmented, and the preserved. From an emulation or a physical hardware standpoint. It's also why I wanted to finish v0.6 before v0.7's goals of safely sourcing DDLs, Torznab, and Newznab. Really prep it for the anything case.


In this development cycle I've been really thankful for my homelab, the non-converged isolated VLAN between Raiden and Motherbase have proven a really effective testing environment. Everything from permission errors in docker, to the LXC, to the mount in the PVE host, to the NAS itself. I can test it before I put a production-ready version out there. That said, I hope that I develop a little bit of a community to test the ARM side of things. I don't currently have a device with ARM support, and considering the start of that was in this most recent version, I am actively seeking people interested in this project with ARM-based nodes to stress-test these binaries hard before they hit the main branch. I want to make something that will last, something that will be of use to the community, something that can grow with the ever changing needs of the video game preservation community.


I kick myself sometimes thinking about it, but I really did look at Sonarr & Radarr, some of the most successful, polished, functional automation suites in the homelab world and "I'm going to build that, but for the one medium that is notoriously fragmented, protected by encryption, and lacks a unified metadata standard."


That said, the process of learning has been quite fulfilling and enjoyable, though I do hope it's not a solo-project forever. It's been a two-week sprint in the development, and I hope that momentum doesn't drop. I will take some time to focus on the "real world" again instead of getting lost in the code and development. Though, I can comfortably say this has made it's way into my daily routine.


>> The Plans For Gamearr

On the github repo I do talk about the various development stages and what the overarching goal for each alpha/beta version will be. The current version v0.5-alpha focuses on NAS stability, reliability, file integrity, and preserving your physical drives, while also including ARM support as more of a bonus for the users with Synology NAS or a Raspberry Pi. v0.6 like I mentioned mainly focuses on expanding the metadata, parsing, and decryption of pre-ninth generation platforms. v0.7 works on incorportating Torznab and Newznab sources, hopefully having it perfectly integrate with Prowlarr, while keeping TSV sources for PSV, PSP, and PSX and introducing the DDL parsing the XML used there. In v0.8 I'll be trying to get PC, and ninth gen support integrated, one of the biggest features I'm looking foward to is auto-pulling from the Steam Wishlist much like Sonarr/Radarr have the watchlist. v0.9 will mostly be polishing up, especially in regards to the downloading process. I'm not sure exactly how I'll do it yet, but make sure that no bad rips, malicious files, what have you, make it into your downloads folder, or especially to your NAS. And then after all of that hopefully v1.0 will see the light of day.
