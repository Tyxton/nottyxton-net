Managing my homelab from 800+ miles away.

[ SITUATION ]

The entirety of MSF is hosted in Houston, TX. Right now, due to life events and unforseen circumstances, I am in an extended stay in my hometown in Tennessee. The distance between me and my homelab is roughly 850 miles and a 15-Hour drive. Thankfully, I had everything prepped long before this happened, but I'm going to go over everything I have in place to ensure stability and my 99.99% uptime philosphy, as well as security from this great distance.

[ WIREGUARD_TUNNEL ]

Long before I moved, I had setup a wireguard (wg-easy) container on ops-core-01 setup to be able to communicate across 10.20.0.0, 10.30.0.0, and 10.40.0.0. I had also generated the configuration files and configured Snake, Otacon, and Codec in advance to have this WG tunnel in place.


I did end up changing this, however, for ease of use, configuration, and network-wide access without punching pinholes into ops-core-01; I opted to use UniFi's built in Wireguard VPN server. This let's me login to the UniFi site from any device down the line, create a new client, download or scan the VPN configuration file, and immediately have my tunnel up. Instead of needing an open tunnel to add a new client. This also removes the dependency of my ops-nodes for the VPN, if I need to get into the network to fix ops-core-01, the VPN inside would be inaccessible.

[ DNS ]

I configured AdGuard Home to also allow traffic in from the new VPN subnet, I used 10.80.0.0. This allows me to use DNS remotely without inheretly being on my Trusted VLAN, this is more secure for my tunnels and allows me to tell exactly where all traffic is coming from, if the tunnel leaks and unwanted traffic is noticed, I could tell it was coming from the VPN VLAN immediately upon seeing the 10.80.0.x. Now, something like `grafana.local.nottyxton.net` pulls up my grafana dashboard without opening it up to WAN. There is absolutely no reason I should ever have my admin-access pages open to WAN, this logic applies to services like Immich as well, the less I advertise the better, if it does not need to be public, it is not.

[ THINKPAD_T440 ]

I did bring along my Thinkpad T440 initially I didn't have an inherent use for it in mind, but I figured a spare compute node may prove handy out here. While dated, this Thinkpad is what ran the original tyxton.net website years ago. I have decided to re-purpose it as an always-on "gateway" for the homelab. By that I mean, using it as a secondary ntfy relay server - able to detect and notify me if it detects MSF is down (thanks to a script I will document seperately). I hope to do more with it soon, but this is all it acts as currently. Make sure I can still get my necessary alerts and events from a great distance away without using their cloud-service.

[ MAINTENANCE ]

This is one of the biggest concerns for remote management, be it a remote server cluster, co-lo, etc. If anything physically goes wrong, I will be in the dark until ntfy lets me know and I will be unable to service the machines running MSF. Thankfully, the servers are still running in my apartment in Houston, where my roommate's are still actively in-and-out. While not homelabbers, sysadmins, NOC technicians, they are still technologically literate. I can communicate what needs to be done remotely and they will be able to service them. If I drive fails on Motherbase, my roommate can hotswap it and I can get it re-shimmed within minutes of failure, rather than hours on a flight. Time is precious in these matters, so having a set of hands ready is a great benefit to the current situation.


This has come in handy already, and I've only been away for a week and a half. The power flickered in the apartment, cutting power to the UniFi Stack, Raiden, and Motherbase. Thankfully, I have them configured to come back online healthily and in an order than won't cause Stale File Handles, or other dependency-related issues. There is a unique behavior with Motherbase, if it shuts down suddenly, when it comes back online the fans max out the RPM and hang through the boot and then will smooth back out to their (relatively) quiet state. My roomate noticed the fans spinning harder and louder than usual and called me immediately. Thanks to this immediate phone-call I now know that my UPS is not functioning properly and was able to replace it the same day, preventing anymore hard-shutdowns.


I have also left them with safe-shutdown instructions for if the power goes out, to ensure that hard shutdowns never happen, especially with 14 hard drives nearly constantly spinning on Motherbase.

[ CLOSING_THOUGHTS ]

While remote management, can be stressful, with these systems in place, it ensures a smooth transition and workflow from afar. I hope to be back in HTX soon to continue tinkering live.
